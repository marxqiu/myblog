Opening Question
Does every shape have a perimeter you can measure?
Consider the coastline of an island. Measured with a 1 km ruler: 5.42 km. Measured with a 500 m ruler: 6.93 km. Measured with a 200 m ruler: 8.62 km. Measured with a tiny triangle ruler over three days: 120.87 km. The smaller the ruler, the longer the coastline — and it never stops growing.

This is the Coastline Paradox. Before you learn about perimeter today, you are going to investigate a shape that has the same problem.

4
What Makes a Polygon Different?
Koch Snowflake — Fractal Boundary
Zoom in on any segment of the boundary and you find more jagged bumps. There is always more detail hiding at smaller scales. The "perimeter" you measure depends entirely on how finely you look.
Perimeter at iteration n = 27 × (4/3)ⁿ → ∞
Square — Polygon Boundary
Zoom in on any side of a square and you find… a straight line. There is no hidden detail. A 1 cm ruler and a 1 mm ruler give exactly the same total length for the same side.
Perimeter = 4 × side. The same, always.
The Key Question
Q5. A polygon has sides that are perfectly straight line segments. Explain, in one or two sentences, why this means a polygon always has a unique, well-defined perimeter — unlike a fractal or a coastline.
Target answer: A straight line segment has no hidden detail — it looks exactly the same no matter how closely you examine it. This means every ruler, no matter how small, gives the same measurement. So the perimeter of a polygon is a single, unchanging number. This is what makes polygons so useful in mathematics: their boundaries are exactly measurable.
🌐 Going Further — Fractal Dimension
The mathematician Benoît Mandelbrot (1967) asked: if a coastline is not quite a 1-dimensional line and not quite a 2-dimensional area, what dimension is it? He invented fractal dimension to answer this. A more "crinkled" boundary has a higher fractal dimension — closer to 2. The Koch snowflake has fractal dimension log(4)/log(3) ≈ 1.26.

Q1: The boundary becomes more jagged/crinkled at every scale. No segment stays straight — each is replaced by a bent path. Yet the overall shape still looks like a snowflake (self-similar).

Q2: Ratio = 4/3 ≈ 1.33 each time. Since 4/3 > 1, multiplying by it repeatedly means the perimeter grows without bound → no, it never stops. Formula: P_n = 27 × (4/3)ⁿ → ∞ as n → ∞.

Q3: The area is bounded because the bumps fit inside a circle — the snowflake never escapes its bounding box. But the boundary itself becomes infinitely wiggly, packing more and more length into the same region. Length ≠ area.

Q4: A real coastline has no unique, objective length. The measured length depends on the ruler size chosen. There is no "true" perimeter — only an answer relative to a chosen scale of measurement.

Transition to lesson: "A polygon's sides are perfectly straight — no hidden bumps at any scale. So its perimeter is always the same number. This is exactly why we study polygon perimeter: it is a measurement we can trust. Now let's learn how to calculate it."

Common confusion: Students may think a finer ruler always gives a more accurate answer (true for smooth curves like circles). Emphasize that fractals are different: they have structure at every scale, so finer measurement reveals new length, not just more precision.
